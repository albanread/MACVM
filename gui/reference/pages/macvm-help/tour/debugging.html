Introspection & Reliability

A virtual machine you can trust for real work has to be dependable as well as fast — and dependability does not come from heroics in a debugger after the fact. It comes from building so that failures are caught early, and from having the tools to prove the machine is behaving. That proof is what this page is about, and MACVM invests heavily in it. Self and Strongtalk were pioneers of deep, live introspection into a running system; MACVM carries that spirit into its own tooling and leans on it hard.

A debugger that survives the fault

MACVM ships a layered debugger, DBG0 through DBG5, built for a machine where interpreted frames and compiled native frames are stacked on top of one another. Its features climb from "what happened" to "why the machine emitted this exact instruction":

  • a PROBE crash dossier — on a fault it captures the registers and a full mixed-tier backtrace, walking interpreter and compiled frames as one stack. It is written to survive a foreign fault: a wild memory access down in JIT-compiled code does not take the process out silently — it hands you the scene;

  • breakpoints and a mixed-tier backtrace you can ask for on demand, so a hot compiled method and a cold interpreted one read as a single call chain;

  • an arm64 disassembler and IR dumps, for reading the compiler's actual output — the intermediate form on the way in, and the AArch64 the assembler emitted on the way out;

  • step-between-calls, to advance the machine one send at a time across the tier boundary.

Tracing channels

Below the debugger sits a set of always-available tracing channels, switched on through the MACVM_TRACE environment variable. Each answers a different "what is the engine doing right now" question, and they compose:

  • bytecode and count — the interpreter's fetch–decode stream, and how often each site runs;

  • gc, jit and deopt — collections, compilations, and every deoptimization with its reason;

  • dnu, calls, oops and stats — does-not-understand dispatch, the call trail, object references, and a running summary.

The differential discipline

All of that is instrumentation. The thing that actually keeps MACVM stable is a rule, and it is simple enough to state in one sentence: the interpreter is the oracle, and the compiler is not allowed to disagree with it. The simple dispatch-based interpreter is small enough to trust by inspection. So it is treated as the definition of what the program means, and the optimizing compiler — with all its inline caches, customization, inlining, deoptimization and on-stack replacement — is held to producing byte-identical output to it. Not "close enough". Identical.

Every JIT change is gated against that equality, and against GC and deoptimization stress matrices on top of it: the same work run while the scavenger and the full compacting collector move frames underneath live compiled code, and while speculation is deliberately broken to force deopts. A change that makes a benchmark faster but shifts a single bit, or that survives a normal run but not a run under moving-GC stress, does not land. This is unglamorous and it is the entire reason the earlier pages could quote their numbers without an asterisk.

A VM held bit-for-bit to a trusted oracle, under a collector that moves the ground beneath it, is a machine you can build on with confidence. That is the goal — and the next page sets out plainly what MACVM keeps from Strongtalk, what it drops, and what is new.
